Blockchain & Crypto

Molly Murray


Cryptocurrencies are a type of digital currency which allows people to make payments directly to each other through a decentralised online system. This system is called the blockchain, which is a digitally distributed, public ledger that exists across a network, recording and storing information in a way that makes it almost impossible to change, cheat or hack the system. The blockchain does not store any of its information in a central location; instead, the data is copied and spread across a range of networks that make up the blockchain.

Cryptocurrencies are generated through a process called mining, which uses computing power to help validate transactions by solving complex cryptographic hash puzzles which add blocks to the blockchain. Every computer on the network will then update to reflect this change. The way cryptocurrencies are valued depends on what people are willing to pay for them in the market, as they have no legislated or intrinsic value. As a result of this, the worth of these digital assets fluctuates constantly.

The blockchain continues to become more competitive as new ideas, currencies and technologies are introduced to the space, but in terms of cryptocurrencies, Ethereum proves to be of the state of the art of current blockchain technologies. Ethereum was the second largest coin created after bitcoin, with improvements in the fundamental design, which made its processes significantly more efficient as the technology continued to improve.

A recent study shows that 76% of financial institutions intend to use cryptocurrencies in the next three years; however, a new technology that experts are interested in seeing grow over the next few years is NFTs. Non-fungible tokens exist on a blockchain and are created using the same type of programming for cryptocurrencies. These unique digital tokens recently took off, with many tech investors seeing huge profits but experts are still sceptical about if this bubble will eventually burst. However, NFTs still have the potential to be infinitely valuable in a number of industries, increasing security and processing costs for transactions. The blockchain is a combination of three leading technologies, cryptographic keys and a peer-to-peer network that contains a shared ledger. This is a means of computing that stores the records and transactions of the network. The technology has constantly been evolving since the creation of bitcoin, developing new and unique properties. It was created with the help of bringing together multiple existing technologies and assembling them in a new way; those included concepts that involved cryptography, game theory, algorithms, and economics.

Cryptocurrencies can have a significant impact on the economy, with past examples suggesting that harnessing crypto effectively can reap economic benefits through investment, innovation, jobs, and taxes. From a business perspective adopting crypto as a digital asset can give businesses access to new demographics and make their technical and financial management systems more efficient.

As official government entities work out a legal framework and taxation system for cryptocurrencies and the blockchain, it is possible they could eventually find their way into the wallets of consumers on a large scale. Although Bitcoin is now an official currency in the Central African Republic, the transactions have been made illegal in China, so global unity on united crypto regulation seems unlikely, at least for the short term. The Australian government has recently announced a significant step toward regulating bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies as we are set to be the first country to do a virtual stocktake of the cryptocurrency sector. They will initially be focusing on grouping types of crypto assets which has been called the "first step in a reform agenda".

The people that will most likely be significantly impacted are those that work in relevant jobs and industries to cryptocurrencies, such as finance and business. One of the most consequential impacts of the rise of cryptocurrencies and blockchain technology on the job market will be the shift from traditional finance and banking to financial technology or fintech, which may make many of the more conventional banking jobs redundant. As the blockchain is centralised and runs and exists from the user's computer, the system is much simpler and does not require as much, if any, assistance from a third party.

Cryptocurrencies and the blockchain will most likely not have a significant effect on me for at least a few years until crypto is more regulated and considered a mainstream currency. However, the success of cryptocurrencies such as bitcoin and Ethereum and whether their value increases or decreases will affect me as I purchased small amounts of them around five years ago when I first learned about the blockchain.

If crypto became a regulated and mainstream currency, it would impact my daily life in terms of how I pay for things, send money to people, and travel. My money would most likely not be kept in a bank as people who own cryptocurrencies usually store them in some form of wallet, which can be online or offline. Travelling would also be very different; a centralised currency would mean exchange rates between other foreign currencies would not exist. Cash would also most likely not be used as all transactions would be made and documented digitally. When purchasing things and sending money to friends, I would also send money directly to their accounts; therefore, EFTPOS fees and bank fees would be significantly reduced.

This will be different for me as I am not used to using crypto in everyday life and would probably struggle with the currency's instability. I also don't entirely trust crypto and prefer to keep my savings in a bank account or cash rather than in a crypto wallet. This technology would impact those of my family and friends who work in the field of finance as this would most likely involve a global redesign of finance systems everywhere, thereby making a considerable change to the economy and the finance industry. The blockchain is also a concept that can be very difficult to understand and has a lot of stigma surrounding privacy and security, so it would be a huge adjustment for my friends and family, and specifically for older family members, to adapt to a new system.
